## One-Sentence Thesis

The most consequential events in complex domains may be rare, high-impact, poorly predicted in advance, and then falsely made obvious in hindsight.

## The Book's Role In The Skill

This book gives the skill its epistemic posture:

- distrust models that exclude decisive events,
- separate absence of evidence from evidence of absence,
- notice hindsight explanations,
- protect against unknown unknowns,
- design for robustness and positive exposure instead of relying on prediction.

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Meaning | Diagnostic question | Use when |
|---|---|---|---|
| Black Swan | A surprise outside regular expectations with large impact and hindsight explainability. | What event would dominate the outcome while being absent from the plan? | Markets, history, product shifts, technology, reputation, policy. |
| Hindsight distortion | After an event, people retrofit causes and reduce perceived randomness. | Was this known beforehand or merely narrated afterward? | Postmortems, market commentary, biographies. |
| Unknown unknowns | Important risks may not be listed in the model. | Does the plan survive events we failed to name? | Strategic planning and risk reviews. |
| Epistemic arrogance | Confidence exceeds actual knowledge. | What would make this model embarrassingly incomplete? | Expert forecasts, dashboards, forecasts, macro narratives. |
| Scalable/extreme domains | Some domains permit one winner/event to dominate. | Can one observation change the whole distribution? | Startups, creators, finance, technology, public narratives. |

## Black Swan Diagnostic

Ask:

1. What events are treated as impossible because they have not happened recently?
2. Which assumption, if false, ruins the plan?
3. Where does historical data fail to include the relevant event?
4. What hidden dependency causes cascade failure?
5. What would be "obvious" only after it happens?
6. Which actor can change rules suddenly?
7. Does the plan require stable correlation, liquidity, law, platform access, or social trust?
8. Is the user short volatility: small gains most days, rare catastrophic loss?

## Decision Moves

- Replace "What will happen?" with "What happens to us if we are wrong?"
- Cap downside before seeking upside.
- Separate forecasts from payoffs: a poor forecast may still be useful if the payoff is convex; a good forecast can still be dangerous if the payoff is concave.
- Prefer actions that remain acceptable under multiple futures.
- Keep slack, redundancy, and exit options.
- In positive Black Swan domains, create many small exposures with capped loss.

## Practical Output For A User

When applying this book, include:

- `Model boundary`: what the current plan assumes away.
- `Tail scenario`: named possible shocks plus allowance for unnamed shocks.
- `Ruin channel`: how surprise becomes irreversible damage.
- `Robustness move`: what protects survival.
- `Optionality move`: what keeps upside open.

## Misuse Warnings

- Do not call any rare event a Black Swan if it was predictable from known exposure.
- Do not use unpredictability as a reason to do nothing.
- Do not confuse "cannot predict exactly" with "cannot prepare structurally."
- Do not claim a precise Black Swan probability without a credible model and source.
- Do not overfit the last disaster into the next risk plan.
