# Future Playbooks

Use this file when the user asks for analysis in a concrete domain.

## Universal Playbook

For any future decision:

1. State the decision.
2. Identify ruin.
3. Map payoff.
4. Check incentives.
5. Convert uncertainty into small tests.
6. Preserve upside only after survival is protected.
7. Define stop conditions.
8. Review process after outcome.

## Investing Playbook

Use when evaluating stocks, crypto, funds, structured products, trading strategies, yield products, or private deals.

### Default Diagnosis

Most investment decisions are fat-tail leaning, path-dependent, and incentive-heavy.

### Must Ask

- What is the maximum loss?
- Can losses exceed initial capital?
- Is there leverage, liquidation, lockup, or liquidity mismatch?
- Who controls custody?
- What happens if the platform/exchange/counterparty fails?
- Does historical performance include dead strategies?
- Does the strategy sell tail risk for steady income?
- Is return driven by skill, beta, leverage, liquidity, or hidden optionality?
- Who earns fees, and what do they lose if it fails?

### Default Moves

- Avoid unbounded downside.
- Position-size by survivability, not excitement.
- Separate main assets from experimental assets.
- Treat yield with no visible risk as a red flag.
- Prefer capped downside with asymmetric upside.
- Never let one thesis end the game.

### Verdict Guide

- `Avoid`: opaque custody, leverage, unbounded loss, fake stability, misaligned seller.
- `Small experiment`: asymmetric upside, capped loss, uncertain thesis.
- `Barbell`: most capital protected, small allocation to high-upside risk.
- `Commit`: rare; requires transparency, aligned incentives, and survival under stress.

## Startup And Product Playbook

Use when evaluating a product idea, startup, feature, go-to-market, or business model.

### Default Diagnosis

Startup outcomes are fat-tailed. The goal is not to plan perfectly, but to generate real feedback while preserving runway.

### Must Ask

- What is the smallest version that touches reality?
- What evidence would show real demand?
- Is demand paid, repeated, or merely verbal?
- What single dependency can kill us: platform, channel, supplier, founder, regulation, data?
- Is the upside scalable?
- Are we building before learning?
- Is burn rate converting uncertainty into fragility?
- Who has skin in the game?

### Default Moves

- Build smaller.
- Ship sooner.
- Sell before scaling.
- Keep runway.
- Avoid one-channel dependence.
- Design experiments with fixed loss and clear learning.
- Let winners scale; kill weak bets without identity drama.

### Verdict Guide

- `Redesign`: big build before demand evidence.
- `Small experiment`: uncertain idea with cheap test.
- `Barbell`: stable income/core business plus exploratory product bets.
- `Commit`: repeated demand, strong retention, scalable upside, survivable downside.

## Career Playbook

Use when deciding whether to change jobs, quit, start a company, study a new skill, move cities, or take a risky opportunity.

### Default Diagnosis

Career decisions mix reputation, optionality, income floor, and path dependence. Prestige is not the same as robustness.

### Must Ask

- What is the downside if this fails?
- Can I return or pivot?
- What options does this create?
- What options does this close?
- Is the upside capped or open?
- Am I taking irreversible downside for someone else's upside?
- Does this build durable skill, network, ownership, or reputation?
- Who is giving advice, and do they bear my constraints?

### Default Moves

- Keep an income floor while testing high-upside paths.
- Build portable skills.
- Prefer opportunities that create future optionality.
- Avoid reputation risks with capped upside.
- Do not confuse comfort with robustness or drama with courage.

### Verdict Guide

- `Avoid`: irreversible downside, capped upside, bad incentives.
- `Small experiment`: side project, short trial, advisory role, limited commitment.
- `Barbell`: stable base plus public experiments/new skills.
- `Commit`: downside survivable, upside meaningful, evidence real.

## Partnership And Hiring Playbook

Use when choosing cofounders, employees, agencies, consultants, vendors, KOLs, or advisors.

### Default Diagnosis

Partnership risk is incentive risk. Competence without aligned downside can still be dangerous.

### Must Ask

- What do they gain if this succeeds?
- What do they lose if this fails?
- Can they exit before damage appears?
- Are they paid for activity, complexity, or outcome?
- Have they survived the consequences of their own advice?
- Do they communicate uncertainty honestly?
- Is their past success repeatable or survivorship-biased?

### Default Moves

- Start with small reversible collaboration.
- Use milestones.
- Avoid large upfront trust.
- Put incentives in writing.
- Require ownership of consequences.
- Watch behavior under small stress.

### Verdict Guide

- `Avoid`: upside-only advisor, complexity seller, no accountability, pressure tactics.
- `Small experiment`: useful but unproven collaborator.
- `Commit`: repeated evidence, aligned incentives, trustworthy under stress.

## Learning And Research Playbook

Use when deciding what to study, what skill to build, or how to research a field.

### Default Diagnosis

Learning is antifragile when each small attempt creates feedback, skill, or optionality.

### Must Ask

- Does this skill create future options?
- Is it Lindy-like or fad-like?
- Can I test progress in reality?
- Does learning compound?
- Is the domain fat-tailed, where being top percentile matters?
- What should I stop consuming?

### Default Moves

- Build projects, not just notes.
- Use reality tests.
- Study foundations plus small frontier bets.
- Keep a public or private artifact trail.
- Remove low-signal information.
- Prefer skills with multiple future uses.

### Verdict Guide

- `Avoid`: passive consumption with no output.
- `Small experiment`: 2-week project, tutorial-to-artifact, mini case study.
- `Barbell`: foundational skill plus speculative new domain.
- `Commit`: repeated usefulness across projects.

## Operational Risk Playbook

Use for wallets, accounts, systems, files, suppliers, workflows, security, and business continuity.

### Default Diagnosis

Operations fail through boring single points of failure. Fragility often hides in convenience.

### Must Ask

- What single failure can stop everything?
- What can be stolen, deleted, banned, leaked, or locked?
- Is there backup?
- Has recovery been tested?
- Who has admin power?
- What dependency can change rules?
- Are failures correlated?

### Default Moves

- Back up critical assets.
- Separate experimental and core accounts.
- Use least privilege.
- Test recovery.
- Keep manual fallbacks.
- Reduce irreversible automation.
- Document critical workflows.

### Verdict Guide

- `Avoid`: core assets exposed to untrusted tools or accounts.
- `Redesign`: too many dependencies or no recovery.
- `Commit`: backups, permissions, and fallback tested.

## Content And Creator Playbook

Use for publishing, personal brand, distribution, communities, and social platforms.

### Default Diagnosis

Attention markets are fat-tailed and platform-dependent. The goal is many small shots with protected reputation and owned channels.

### Must Ask

- Is upside scalable?
- Is downside reputationally capped?
- Are we dependent on one platform?
- What content creates durable asset value?
- Are we copying visible winners without seeing failed attempts?
- What can be tested cheaply?

### Default Moves

- Publish many small pieces.
- Protect reputation.
- Build owned distribution.
- Repurpose winners.
- Avoid identity lock-in too early.
- Track process, not just virality.

### Verdict Guide

- `Small experiment`: most content bets.
- `Barbell`: consistent core identity plus high-upside experiments.
- `Avoid`: reputational downside for low upside.

## Review Playbook

Use after any decision outcome.

### Ask

- Did we win because of skill, structure, or luck?
- Did we lose because of bad process or bad draw?
- What alternative histories were plausible?
- Did our guardrails work?
- Was downside truly capped?
- What did we learn that changes the next bet?
- Were incentives aligned?
- Should we scale, repeat, redesign, or stop?
